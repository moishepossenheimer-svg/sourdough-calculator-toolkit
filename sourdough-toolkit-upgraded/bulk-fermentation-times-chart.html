Fermentation guide

Sourdough Bulk Fermentation Times Chart by Temperature

Bulk fermentation is where many sourdough loaves are won or lost. The chart gives you a starting point, and the dough signs tell you when to move on.

Updated June 2026Practical home baking guide

Bulk fermentation timing chart

Bulk fermentation is the first long rise after mixing and folding. It is where dough gains gas, strength and flavour. Timing matters, but it is not controlled by the clock alone. Temperature, starter amount, starter strength, flour and dough hydration all change the speed.

Use this chart as a planning range for dough made with about 20% starter and normal bread flour. Start checking earlier if your starter is very active or your dough is warm.

Dough or room temperatureTypical bulk rangeWhat to expect
18°C7-10 hoursSlow fermentation, long observation window
19°C6.5-9 hoursStill slow; useful for gentle flavour development
20°C5.5-8 hoursCommon cool-kitchen range
21°C5-7 hoursBalanced timing for many home bakes
22°C4-6 hoursActive but manageable
23°C3.5-5.5 hoursCheck often after the first few hours
24°C3-5 hoursFast enough that over-fermentation can sneak up
25°C+2.5-4.5 hoursWarm dough; reduce starter or watch closely

The signs bulk fermentation is ready

The dough should look alive. It will be smoother than when mixed, puffier, and aerated. You may see bubbles along the side or surface. When you jiggle the bowl, the dough should wobble slightly instead of sitting like a dense lump.

Many bakers aim for a percentage rise rather than a fixed time. A 30-50% rise can be enough for warm dough that will continue fermenting during shaping and proofing. A cooler dough may need closer to 50-75%. The exact target depends on your flour, handling and final proof.

Under-fermented dough

Under-fermented dough often feels dense, tight and resistant. It may tear during shaping because it has not relaxed enough. The baked loaf can burst dramatically, have a tight crumb, and show large tunnels surrounded by dense areas.

If this happens, extend bulk next time or keep the dough warmer. Also check starter strength, because weak starter can make a normal schedule too short.

Over-fermented dough

Over-fermented dough can feel fragile, sticky and slack. It may spread after shaping and refuse to hold tension. The surface can look bubbly but weak, and the loaf may bake flatter than expected.

If this happens, reduce bulk time, lower starter percentage, keep the dough cooler, or reduce hydration slightly until the process is easier to control.

Why dough temperature beats room temperature

Room temperature is useful, but dough temperature is better. Flour, water and mixing all affect final dough temperature. A bowl of dough can also stay warmer or cooler than the room for a surprising amount of time.

If you want more repeatable results, take the dough temperature after mixing. Write it down next to the bulk time and the final loaf result. Over a few bakes, you will see your own pattern.

How starter percentage changes the chart

The chart assumes a moderate starter amount. If you use 10% starter, expect bulk to take longer. If you use 30% starter, expect it to move faster. A very active starter can also shorten the range, especially in a warm kitchen.

This is why the bulk fermentation estimator asks about starter strength. It cannot see your dough, but it can help set a sensible first check time.

A simple bulk fermentation method

  1. Mix dough and note the time and temperature.
  2. Perform stretch and folds or coil folds during the first half of bulk.
  3. After the folds, let the dough rise undisturbed.
  4. Start checking near the early end of the chart range.
  5. Shape when the dough is expanded, aerated and still strong enough to hold structure.
The most useful habitTake a photo of the dough just after mixing and another before shaping. Your eye learns faster when you compare real examples from your own kitchen.

Should bulk fermentation double?

Not always. Some recipes tell you to double the dough, but sourdough can over-ferment if you wait for a full 100% rise, especially in a warm kitchen or with high starter percentage. Many successful loaves are shaped before doubling.

If your dough consistently collapses or spreads, try shaping earlier. If your loaf is dense and tight, try giving bulk more time.

Frequently asked questions

How long should sourdough bulk ferment at 21°C?

A dough with about 20% starter often takes around 5-7 hours at 21°C, but starter strength and dough temperature can shift that range.

Should sourdough double during bulk fermentation?

Not always. Many doughs are shaped before doubling, especially warm doughs. Look for expansion, bubbles, a smoother surface and a light jiggle.

What happens if bulk fermentation is too long?

The dough can become slack, sticky and hard to shape. It may spread after shaping and bake into a flatter loaf.
