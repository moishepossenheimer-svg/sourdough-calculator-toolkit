What Is Sourdough Hydration? A Practical Beginner Guide

Hydration is one of the simplest numbers in sourdough, but it explains a lot: why dough feels sticky, why some loaves spread, and why changing 20g of water can transform a bake.

Updated June 2026Practical home baking guide

Hydration in plain English

In sourdough, hydration is the weight of water compared with the weight of flour. A dough made with 500g flour and 350g water is 70% hydration because 350 is 70% of 500. The flour is always treated as 100%, then everything else is measured against it.

This sounds technical, but it is one of the most useful ideas in bread baking. Hydration tells you how wet the dough will feel, how open the crumb might become, how quickly the dough may ferment, and how much skill you will need when shaping.

Quick formulaWater divided by flour, multiplied by 100, gives hydration percentage. For 350g water and 500g flour: 350 divided by 500 times 100 equals 70%.

The best hydration for beginners

Most beginners should start around 65-70% hydration. That range gives enough water for good oven spring and a tender crumb, but it is not so wet that shaping becomes a fight. If your dough spreads like batter, sticks to everything and will not hold tension, the problem may not be your hands. The hydration may simply be too high for your flour or current technique.

Lower hydration is not a failure. A 65% loaf can be excellent: tall, flavourful, sliceable and reliable. Once you can mix, fold, bulk ferment and shape that dough consistently, raising hydration by two or three percentage points is much easier.

Why flour changes everything

Two doughs with the same hydration can behave differently because flour absorbs water differently. Strong bread flour usually handles more water than all-purpose flour. Wholemeal flour absorbs more water than white flour because bran and germ soak up liquid. Freshly milled flour can feel thirsty at first, then slacken as fermentation continues.

This is why copying a high-hydration recipe from a photo can be frustrating. The baker may be using a stronger flour, a cooler room, a different starter and years of shaping practice. Use the number as a clue, not a promise.

How hydration affects fermentation

Wetter dough often ferments faster because microbes move through it more easily and enzymes can act more freely. It can also feel weaker sooner if gluten development is limited. That does not mean high hydration is bad; it means the dough asks for gentler handling and closer attention.

If your dough is warm, wet and made with a large amount of starter, fermentation can move quickly. In that situation, the difference between nicely aerated and over-proofed can be shorter than expected.

How to adjust hydration without ruining a recipe

If a recipe gives you trouble, change water in small steps. For a 500g flour loaf, reducing water by 15g lowers hydration by 3 percentage points. That is enough to feel a real difference without turning the recipe into a different bread.

  • If the dough is unmanageably sticky, reduce hydration by 3-5 points next time.
  • If the loaf is tight and dense but fermentation was good, increase hydration by 2-3 points.
  • If the dough spreads after shaping, look at fermentation and gluten strength before blaming hydration alone.
  • If you add wholemeal flour, be ready to add a little more water or extend the rest after mixing.

Using hydration with a sourdough starter

Many calculators, including the dough calculator, assume a 100% hydration starter. That means the starter contains equal weights of flour and water. In everyday home baking, this assumption is usually close enough.

If you use a very stiff starter, your dough will contain less water than the calculator assumes. If you use a very liquid starter, your dough will contain more. Serious formula writing accounts for the flour and water inside the starter, but most beginner recipes keep things simple by treating starter as its own ingredient.

Frequently asked questions

Is 70% hydration good for sourdough?

Yes. For many home bakers, 70% hydration is a strong everyday target because it can produce a light loaf while still being manageable with normal bread flour.

Why is my 75% hydration dough so sticky?

It may be too wet for your flour, too warm, under-developed, or over-fermented. Try reducing water slightly and adding more folds before moving back up.

Does starter count toward hydration?

Technically yes, because starter contains flour and water. Many beginner calculators assume a 100% hydration starter and keep the formula simple.
