Sourdough Troubleshooting Guide: Dense, Gummy, Flat or Sticky Bread

A disappointing loaf is usually giving you useful information. This guide turns the most common sourdough problems into clear next-bake adjustments.

Updated June 2026Original guide with professional references

Start with the four big variables

Most sourdough problems trace back to starter strength, dough temperature, fermentation level or hydration. Before changing flour brands, buying tools or rewriting the whole recipe, look at those four variables. They explain more failed loaves than almost anything else.

Best next-bake ruleChange one thing at a time. If you reduce hydration, strengthen the starter and shorten bulk all at once, you will not know which change helped.

Sticky dough

Sticky dough can be normal, especially above 72% hydration, but dough that smears, spreads and refuses to hold shape is usually too wet for the flour, too warm, under-developed or over-fermented. Add folds early in bulk, use wet hands, and reduce water by 15-25g in a 500g flour loaf next time.

Dense loaf

Dense sourdough is often under-fermented or made with starter that was not active enough. Look for a starter that rises predictably after feeding, dough that becomes aerated during bulk, and a shaped loaf that has some life before baking. If the crumb is tight all the way through, extend bulk or warm the dough slightly next time.

Gummy crumb

Gummy crumb can come from under-baking, slicing too soon, too much water, or poor fermentation. Bake until the crust is deeply coloured, let the loaf cool fully, and avoid judging crumb while the starches are still setting. If the loaf stays tacky after a full cool, reduce hydration or improve fermentation.

Flat loaf and weak oven spring

A flat loaf can be over-fermented, under-shaped, under-steamed or baked in an oven that was not hot enough. If the dough spread before baking, work on fermentation and shaping. If it looked strong but did not expand, check steam and oven heat.

Large tunnels with dense areas

Big random tunnels surrounded by dense crumb usually point to under-fermentation or shaping that trapped air unevenly. Give the dough more time during bulk, and shape firmly enough to organise the loaf without crushing all the gas.

Frequently asked questions

Why is my sourdough dense?

The most common reasons are weak starter, under-fermentation, low dough temperature or too much degassing during shaping.

Why is my sourdough gummy?

It may be under-baked, sliced too hot, too high in hydration, or poorly fermented. Cool the loaf fully before judging crumb.

Can over-fermented dough be saved?

You can often bake it in a loaf tin or turn it into focaccia. It may not be tall, but it can still taste good.
