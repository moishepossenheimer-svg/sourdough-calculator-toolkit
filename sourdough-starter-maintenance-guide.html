Sourdough Feeding Ratios Explained: 1:1:1, 1:2:2 and 1:5:5

Feeding ratios look cryptic at first, but they are just a shorthand for how much starter, flour and water go into the jar.

Updated June 2026Practical home baking guide

What a feeding ratio means

Sourdough feeding ratios are written as starter : flour : water. A 1:2:2 feed means one part starter, two parts flour and two parts water by weight. If you keep 20g starter, you add 40g flour and 40g water.

The ratio changes how much fresh food the starter receives. More fresh flour and water usually means the starter takes longer to peak and has more food before it becomes acidic again.

RatioExampleSpeedUseful when
1:1:120g starter + 20g flour + 20g waterFastYou need starter soon or the room is cool
1:2:220g starter + 40g flour + 40g waterModerateEveryday feeding and predictable bakes
1:5:510g starter + 50g flour + 50g waterSlowOvernight feeds, warm kitchens, reducing acidity

Why 1:1:1 peaks faster

In a 1:1:1 feed, the existing starter makes up one third of the final mixture. There is a lot of active culture relative to fresh food, so it can rise quickly. This is useful when you want to bake soon, but it also means the starter may pass its peak sooner.

If your starter peaks and collapses before you mix dough, it may still work, but it is less predictable. A collapsed starter is often more acidic and may ferment dough more slowly than a starter used near peak.

Why 1:2:2 is a good everyday ratio

A 1:2:2 feed gives the starter more food and a wider window. It is a sensible default for many kitchens because it is not too fast and not too slow. If you feed in the morning, it may be ready later in the day depending on temperature and starter strength.

This ratio is also easy to scale. Keep 10g starter and add 20g flour plus 20g water for a small maintenance feed. Keep 40g starter and add 80g flour plus 80g water when you need a larger amount for baking.

Why 1:5:5 is useful

A 1:5:5 feed gives the starter a lot of fresh food. It is helpful if your starter becomes acidic too quickly, if your kitchen is warm, or if you want to feed before bed and bake the next morning.

Because there is less seed starter relative to fresh flour, it takes longer to peak. That longer rise can produce a milder, stronger starter when managed well.

How temperature changes the ratio

Temperature and ratio work together. A 1:2:2 feed in a warm 25°C kitchen may peak faster than a 1:1:1 feed in a cool 17°C kitchen. This is why no ratio has a fixed time attached to it.

Instead of memorising times, mark the jar and watch how long your starter takes to double or triple. That observation gives you a personal schedule for your flour, water and room.

How to choose a ratio

  • Choose 1:1:1 when you need a fast refresh and can watch the starter closely.
  • Choose 1:2:2 for normal daily feeding and predictable baking.
  • Choose 1:5:5 when the room is warm, the starter is too acidic, or you need a longer window.
  • Use smaller seed amounts when maintaining starter so you do not waste flour.

Common starter feeding mistakes

The first mistake is feeding by volume. A spoonful of flour and a spoonful of water do not weigh the same, so the starter hydration drifts. Use grams when possible.

The second mistake is keeping too much starter. You rarely need a huge jar. A small starter refreshed well is cheaper, cleaner and easier to manage.

The third mistake is using the starter at a random point. For predictable bread, use it when it is active, expanded and close to peak, not straight from the fridge or long after collapse.

Frequently asked questions

What does 1:2:2 mean for sourdough starter?

It means one part starter, two parts flour and two parts water by weight. For 20g starter, add 40g flour and 40g water.

Which feeding ratio is best?

For everyday use, 1:2:2 is a good default. Use 1:1:1 for speed and 1:5:5 for a longer window.

Should starter be fed by weight?

Yes. Feeding by grams is more consistent than feeding by spoons or cups because flour and water have very different densities.
