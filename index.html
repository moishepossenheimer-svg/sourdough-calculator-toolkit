Starter percentage

How Much Sourdough Starter Should I Use? 10%, 20% and 30% Explained

Starter amount is one of the easiest ways to control sourdough timing. The right amount depends on temperature, starter strength and your schedule.

Updated June 2026Practical home baking guide

The short answer

A good default is 20% starter based on flour weight. If your dough has 500g flour, 20% starter means 100g starter. That amount works for many same-day sourdough schedules at normal room temperature.

But starter percentage is not a moral rule. It is a timing control. More starter usually means faster fermentation. Less starter usually means slower fermentation and, often, a longer window for flavour to develop.

How starter percentage changes the dough

Starter brings yeast, lactic acid bacteria, flour, water and acidity into the dough. When you increase starter, you add more active culture and more prefermented flour. The dough usually rises faster because the microbial population starts larger.

That speed can be useful. If your kitchen is cold or you need a loaf shaped before dinner, 25-30% starter may help. But speed can also make timing harder. A warm dough with 30% starter can over-ferment while you are waiting for a dramatic rise.

When to use less starter

Use less starter when you want more time. A 10-15% starter dough is helpful in summer, in a warm kitchen, or when you want bulk fermentation to stretch through a workday. It can also be useful if your starter is very acidic and you want less acidity in the final dough.

Less starter does not automatically mean a weaker loaf. If the starter is healthy and the dough has enough time, a lower inoculation can produce excellent bread with a clean flavour and good structure.

When to use more starter

Use more starter when conditions are slow. Cool kitchens, cold flour, a young starter or a tight schedule can all justify increasing starter. This is especially helpful for beginner bakers who struggle to see fermentation because their dough barely moves in a cold room.

Just remember that more starter reduces your margin for error. Once the dough is moving, it may be ready sooner than the recipe suggests.

Starter strength matters more than the exact number

A strong 15% starter can outperform a weak 30% starter. The number only helps if the starter is alive, fed and close to peak. A starter that doubles or triples predictably after feeding will ferment dough more reliably than one that has been neglected in the fridge and used cold.

If your dough is consistently slow, refresh the starter for a couple of feeds before changing the recipe. If it smells sharply acidic, feed at a higher ratio such as 1:3:3 or 1:5:5 so the culture gets more fresh food.

Practical ruleChoose starter percentage for your schedule, but end bulk fermentation based on the dough. The percentage starts the plan; the dough finishes it.

How to calculate starter percentage

Starter percentage is starter weight divided by flour weight, then multiplied by 100. For 100g starter and 500g flour, the calculation is 100 divided by 500 times 100, which equals 20%.

If you would rather not do the arithmetic, use the dough calculator. Enter your flour weight, then adjust starter percentage until the amount matches the schedule you want.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best starter amount for beginners?

Start with 20% starter based on flour weight. It is common, easy to calculate and works well for many same-day bakes.

Can I use too much starter?

Yes. A high starter percentage can make dough ferment quickly, especially in a warm room, which increases the risk of over-fermentation.

Can I use only 10% starter?

Yes, if the starter is healthy and the dough has enough time. Ten percent is useful for warm kitchens and slower schedules.
