Real Sourdough vs Fake Sourdough: How to Spot the Difference

As sourdough has become popular, more supermarket loaves use the word without relying on traditional slow fermentation. Here is how to read the clues.

Updated June 2026Original guide with professional references

Start with the ingredient list

Traditional sourdough is built from flour, water, salt and a live starter culture. Add-ins like seeds, olives or dried fruit can be legitimate, but a long list of dough conditioners, preservatives, commercial yeast and acidifiers suggests a shortcut loaf.

Why some loaves imitate sourdough

Slow fermentation takes time, space and skill. Commercial bakeries may use yeast for speed, then add acids or flavourings to imitate tang. That does not make the bread automatically bad, but it is different from naturally leavened sourdough.

What real sourdough tends to look and taste like

Real sourdough often has a chewier crumb, a more developed crust, natural acidity and a shorter shelf life than highly processed bread. It may be mild or tangy depending on the baker; sourness alone is not proof.

Questions to ask a bakery

  • Is the bread naturally leavened with a sourdough starter?
  • Does it contain commercial yeast?
  • How long is the fermentation?
  • What flour is used?
  • Are there dough conditioners or preservatives?

Frequently asked questions

Is supermarket sourdough always fake?

No. Some supermarket loaves are naturally leavened, but many use shortcuts. The ingredient list and bakery information are the best clues.

Does real sourdough contain yeast?

Traditional sourdough is leavened by a starter culture. Some bakeries add tiny amounts of yeast for scheduling, but heavily yeast-raised bread is different.

Is sour flavour enough to prove sourdough?

No. Acidic ingredients can imitate tang. Fermentation method and ingredient list matter more than flavour alone.
